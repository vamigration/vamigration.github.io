Perception of migration on social media - U.S. vs U.K

Migration and social media

70.8 million people are forcibly displaced worldwide for reasons such as poverty or war.

Social media is often a rich source of information to observe and understand the views of the general population towards migrants and refugees: how much the matter concerns them, how they feel about it and what events are perceived as particularly important.


The nearly 351,000 tweets represented as circles shown on the left were collected during the months of August, September and October from the United States and the United Kingdom.

Let's analyze this data to get an idea about the Perception of migration on social media

US and UK tweets

The United States and the United Kingdom are two countries with large foreign populations that have shown strong views regarding migrants and refugees over the years.

The high engagement of people with the topic of migration in the two countries can be seen from the large number of tweets collected: 251,294 for the US and 99,112 for the UK.

Topic engagement

The number of tweets on the matter is around 2.5 times higher in the US than in the UK.

However, considering the number of inhabitants in each country, we see that the people in the United Kingdom are almost 2 times more engaged with the topic than those in the United States.

On the other hand, the original tweets in the U.S. were replicated 1688 times, which means more than 4 million people engaging with the topic in around 3 months. Something similar happens in the UK, where the original tweets were retweeted 1132 times.

In any case, all this shows that migration is an important topic and it's reflected on social media engagement. But, how do people feel about it?

Sentiment

The tweets collected can be broken down showing the people's sentiment when discussing about migrants and refugees: negative, neutral or positive.

The migration-related tweets in the US tend to be more negative rather than positive (with more than 50% negatives versus only 33% positives), whereas in the UK they are evenly balanced (around 42% for both negative and positive).

Hashtags

The hashtags have always been a strong representative of movements on Twitter, and looking at the most popular ones gives us an idea about the topics that people feel strong about in the context of migration, such as #refugeeswelcome and #trump in the US, or #brexit and #brokenpromises in the UK.
